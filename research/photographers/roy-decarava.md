# Roy DeCarava (1919–2009)

Roy DeCarava was an American photographer — the first African-American photographer to receive a Guggenheim Fellowship (1952) — whose photographs of Harlem and of jazz musicians are the foundational body of his work (src-nyt-2009-decarava-obit). Born in Harlem, New York City on 9 December 1919 and died in New York City on 27 October 2009, DeCarava published the 1955 book *The Sweet Flypaper of Life* with Langston Hughes the same year that *The Family of Man* opened at MoMA (src-nyt-2009-decarava-obit).

## Career

After training as a painter and printmaker under the auspices of the WPA and at Cooper Union, DeCarava turned to photography in the late 1940s. His 1952 Guggenheim funded the Harlem photographs that Hughes edited into *The Sweet Flypaper of Life* (Simon and Schuster, 1955) — a fictionalised prose-frame for DeCarava's photographs, and a landmark in African-American photographic publishing. In 1955 DeCarava opened A Photographer's Gallery on 84th Street in New York, one of the earliest photography-specific galleries in the US (src-nyt-2009-decarava-obit).

DeCarava's 1996 retrospective at the Museum of Modern Art, *Roy DeCarava: A Retrospective*, curated by Peter Galassi, cemented his standing in the American photographic canon (src-moma-collection-artists-index).

## Connection to *The Family of Man*

DeCarava has a single plate in the MoMA Master Checklist — #18, in Section 2 Lovers, credited "Roy De Carava, American" (one word form in the checklist) (src-moma-exh-0569-master-checklist). His estate and later institutional literature uses the canonical single-word form "DeCarava"; our `data/photographers.csv` row uses "Roy DeCarava" and records "De Carava" as the checklist spelling variant.

## Perspective notes

DeCarava's participation in *The Family of Man* — alongside his near-simultaneous publication of *The Sweet Flypaper of Life* — is a case where a Black photographer's work enters the 1955 MoMA exhibition on exactly the terms Barthes criticised (dehistoricised, decontextualised, collapsed into universal "love"). Sandeen 1995 and later critical writers take the narrow inclusion of Black photographers (and subjects) in *The Family of Man* as a primary case study in how the exhibition's humanist frame absorbed difference without acknowledging structural inequality.
