# Robert Capa (1913–1954)

Robert Capa — born Endre Ernő Friedmann in Budapest on 22 October 1913 — was a Hungarian-born, American-naturalized photojournalist best known for his combat photography in five wars and as a co-founder of Magnum Photos in 1947 (src-icp-capa-archive). The MoMA Master Checklist for *The Family of Man* records his nationality as "American" per his US naturalization; the scholarly convention at the International Center of Photography and in the standard Richard Whelan biography is "Hungarian-American" (src-icp-capa-archive).

## Career

Capa adopted the working name "Robert Capa" in Paris in the mid-1930s. His Spanish Civil War coverage for *Vu* and *Regards* made his reputation; his 1944 D-Day landing photographs for *LIFE* on Omaha Beach became some of the most-reproduced images of the Second World War (src-icp-capa-archive). In 1947 he co-founded Magnum Photos with Henri Cartier-Bresson, David "Chim" Seymour, George Rodger, and William Vandivert — a cooperative agency owned by its photographers and giving them copyright and editorial control over their work (src-magnum-photographer-bios).

Capa was killed by a land mine on 25 May 1954 near Thai-Binh, Indochina, while on assignment for *LIFE* covering the final weeks of the First Indochina War (src-nyt-1954-capa-obit).

## Connection to *The Family of Man*

One Capa print appears in the MoMA Master Checklist, at plate #26 (Section 3, Marriage), attributed to "Robert Capa, Magnum, American — Czechoslovakia" (src-moma-exh-0569-master-checklist). Because Capa died nine months before the exhibition opened on 24 January 1955 (and shortly after his fellow Magnum member Werner Bischof — see `research/photographers/werner-bischof.md` when written), his contribution is posthumous. Steichen's curatorial assistant Wayne Miller and Steichen himself had extensive working relationships with Magnum through 1953–54.

After Robert Capa's death, his younger brother Cornell Capa began the organizational work that led to the founding of the International Center of Photography (ICP) in New York in 1974, in part to preserve Robert Capa's archive (src-icp-capa-archive).

## Perspective notes

The MoMA Master Checklist's "American" attribution reflects the checklist's 1955 convention of listing the photographer's current nationality rather than country of birth. Recording Capa in our data as "American" and flagging "Hungarian-American" in notes preserves the primary-source form while not erasing the biographical reality that the "Robert Capa" persona was constructed by a refugee European photographer working in Paris, London, and New York.
